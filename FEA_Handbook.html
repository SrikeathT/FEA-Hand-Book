Section CBasics of Meshing

Meshing discretizes a continuous geometric domain into finite smaller subdomains called elements. This forms the basis for numerical analysis in FEA.

Mesh Types

Structured Mesh

Elements arranged in a regular, organized pattern aligned with the geometry's coordinate system. Typically used for simple geometries and offers high quality elements.

Unstructured Mesh

Elements arranged in an irregular pattern, allowing flexibility for complex geometries. More automated but can result in lower quality elements.

Element Shapes

8 NODES
Hexahedral (Brick)
High accuracy and efficiency. Ideal for geometries that can be swept or mapped.
3D
4 NODES
Tetrahedron (Tet)
Highly versatile for complex geometries. Patch conforming or patch independent.
3D
6 NODES
Prism / Wedge
Used in boundary layers or transitions between hex and tet meshes.
3D
5 NODES
Pyramidal
Transition elements between hexahedral and tetrahedral meshes.
3D

Mesh Density

Coarse
Larger elements, lower accuracy, shorter computation time, fewer resources.
Medium
Balanced element size, moderate accuracy and computation time.
Fine
Smaller elements, higher accuracy, longer computation time, more resources.

Tetrahedral Meshing: PC vs PI

Feature Patch Conforming (PC) Patch Independent (PI)
Description Conforms to underlying CAD geometry faces Independent of CAD topology, simplifies complex geometries
Purpose High-quality mesh for well-defined geometries, preserves geometric features Suitable for dirty or complex CAD, can ignore small features and gaps
Advantages Better accuracy for stress concentrations, good for detailed analysis Robust for complex models, faster meshing for problematic geometries
Disadvantages Can fail on dirty CAD, sensitive to small geometric details May not capture fine geometric details, less accurate in some areas

Meshing Controls

  • Sizing — Controls global or local element size. Smaller sizes lead to finer meshes.
  • Inflation — Creates high-aspect-ratio element layers near boundaries for boundary layer effects and contact analysis.
  • Contact Sizing — Refines mesh near contact regions to improve contact pressure and stress accuracy.
  • Refinement — Locally reduces element size in high stress gradient areas without excessively increasing total element count.

E
Section EStatic Structural
Geometry→ Model→ Fixed Support→ Loads→ Solve→ Check S-N Curve
  • Define geometry and material properties.
  • Apply fixed supports to constrain rigid body motion.
  • Apply loads (forces, pressures).
  • Solve the static structural problem.
  • Crucially, compare maximum stresses with the material's S-N curve to assess fatigue life.
F
Section FStatic Structural Linear
Geometry→ Mesh→ Earth Gravity→ Support→ Load Conditions→ Solve→ Solution
  • Model geometry and generate mesh.
  • Apply earth gravity if relevant.
  • Define supports to constrain movement.
  • Specify linear load conditions.
  • Solve for displacements, stresses, and strains.
G
Section GStatic Structural Non-Linear
Material Properties→ Geometry→ Materials→ Earth Gravity→ Load→ Support→ Solve→ Solution
  • Define non-linear material properties (e.g., plasticity).
  • Model geometry and assign materials.
  • Apply earth gravity and non-linear loads.
  • Define supports.
  • Solve the non-linear problem, often iteratively.

Section HLarge Deflection

  • Apply transient loads.
  • Include earth gravity.
  • Enable large deflection analysis in settings.
  • Solve for structural response under large deformations.
I
Section IModal (Natural Frequency)
6 DOF→ Fixed Supports Only→ Resonance Check→ Solve→ Deformation / Stress / Strain→ 6 Modes→ Mode Shape Results
  • A structure has 6 degrees of freedom (3 translational, 3 rotational) at each node.
  • Apply fixed supports to define boundary conditions for modal analysis.
  • Resonance occurs when natural frequency matches applied force frequency — causing large amplitude vibrations.
  • Solve to obtain natural frequencies and corresponding mode shapes.
  • Typically the first 6 modes are analyzed.
  • Right-click on graph → Create Mode Shape Results to visualize.
J
Section JHarmonic Response
6 DOF→ Harmonic links to Modal→ Supports→ Analysis Settings→ Range & Interval→ Solve→ Frequency Range
  • Harmonic analysis often follows modal analysis, using its results.
  • Define supports and apply harmonically varying loads.
  • Specify a frequency range and interval in analysis settings.
  • Solve to determine steady-state response (displacement, stress) over the frequency range.
NOTE: Range 0–15 Hz with interval 15 → results every 1 Hz. To avoid specific frequencies, convert to RPM and ensure they are outside the operating range.
K
Section KSteady State Thermal
Material→ Geometry→ Mesh (Multiphase)→ Temperature→ Convection→ Radiation→ Solve→ Solution
  • Define material thermal properties.
  • Model geometry and generate mesh (consider multiphase if needed).
  • Apply temperature boundary conditions.
  • Define convection (stagnant air) and radiation conditions.
  • Solve to find the steady-state temperature distribution.
L
Section LThermal Expansion Static Structural
Static Structural→ Geometry→ Mesh→ Displacement x/y/z=0→ Thermal Load→ Solve→ Solution
  • Perform a static structural analysis.
  • Model geometry and mesh.
  • Apply displacement constraints (fix movement in certain directions) to simulate thermal expansion.
  • Apply thermal loads (temperature changes).
  • Solve to determine stresses and deformations due to thermal expansion.
M
Section MEigenvalue Buckling
Geometry→ Static Structural→ Fixed Support→ Load Condition→ Eigenvalue Buckling→ Modes→ Results
  • Start with a static structural setup.
  • Apply fixed supports and compressive load conditions.
  • Perform eigenvalue buckling analysis.
  • Output includes buckling modes and load multipliers.
Euler F = (n · π² · E · I) / L² n=mode, E=Young's Modulus, I=Area Moment of Inertia, L=length (pinned-pinned)
NOTE: Force Allowable = Force Applied × Load Multiplier
N
Section NRotor Dynamics (Undamped)
Geometry→ Slice Geometry→ Modal→ Point Mass (MOI)→ Remote Points→ Contact (Bearings)→ K11, K22→ Coriolis + Campbell ON→ Tabular RPM→ Campbell Diagram
  • Prepare geometry with slicing as needed.
  • Perform modal analysis; define point masses for rotating bodies and promote to remote points.
  • Specify bearing contact types with stiffness K11, K22.
  • Damping: OFF · Coriolis effect + Rotor Dynamics (Campbell): ON.
  • Define rotational velocity axis with tabular data.
  • Generate Campbell diagram to identify critical speeds.
  • Visualize mode shapes for modes 1 and 2.
O
Section ORotor Dynamics (Damped)
Geometry + Slice→ Modal→ Point Mass → Remote Points→ K11, K22, C11, C22→ Mesh→ Damped: YES→ Coriolis + Campbell ON→ Solve
  • Similar to undamped but include damping coefficients C11, C22.
  • Damped: YES in analysis settings.
  • Damping control must be greater than stiffness for stability.
  • Solve to analyze the damped response.
Stability: Stability value determines if rotor is stable.
Modal Damp Ratio = (actual / critical) × Dampness
Campbell diagram still used to find critical speeds.
P
Section PUnbalanced Harmonic Response
  • Connect harmonic response to a preceding modal analysis.
  • Define geometry with slicing; create points/lines for bearing locations.
  • Apply point masses for rotating bodies and promote to remote points.
  • Define bearing contact types with tabular coefficients for RPM, stiffness K11/K22, damping C11/C22.
  • Enable damping, Coriolis effect, and Campbell in analysis settings.
  • Set up harmonic response with defined frequency range and full solution method.
  • Apply rotating forces — define mass, radius, and hit points at remote points.
  • Solve and analyze frequency response; probe results at bearing locations.

Section QTransient Structural Analysis
Geometry→ Split Faces (Imprints)→ Mesh→ Time = 1/sub steps→ Fixed Support→ Load Conditions (Tabular)→ Solve→ Results
  • Prepare geometry, splitting faces for detailed analysis (imprints).
  • Generate mesh.
  • Configure analysis settings, specifying time steps (1/sub steps).
  • Apply fixed supports and time-dependent load conditions (tabular).
  • Solve to obtain structural response over time.

  • Prepare geometry with split faces (imprints).
  • Generate mesh; configure time steps.
  • Apply time-dependent thermal load conditions (tabular).

Section SHarmonic Spectrum Analysis
Geometry→ Earth Gravity→ Static Structural→ Modal→ Response Spectrum→ Base Excitations→ Direction (Vibration Axis)→ Tabular→ Solve
  • Start with geometry and apply earth gravity.
  • Perform static structural and modal analyses.
  • Set up response spectrum analysis with base excitations.
  • Define boundary conditions and vibrational axis.

T
Section TExplicit Analysis
Explicit Analysis→ Geometry→ Materials→ Mesh→ Material Assignment→ Fixed Support→ Initial Conditions→ Solve→ Solution
  • Set up an explicit dynamics analysis.
  • Define geometry, materials, and mesh; assign materials to parts.
  • Apply fixed supports and initial conditions (velocity, acceleration).
  • Configure analysis settings for explicit solution.
  • Solve for highly non-linear, short-duration events (impacts, crashes, explosions).
U
Section UTopology / Structural Optimization
Static Structural→ Geometry→ Mesh + Supports + Loads→ Solve → Stress Results→ Structural Optimization→ Response Constraints→ Solution
  • Start with static structural analysis.
  • Define geometry, mesh, supports, and loading conditions.
  • Solve for initial stress results.
  • Initiate topology/structural optimization.
  • Define response constraints (mass reduction, stiffness).
  • Solve to find optimized material distribution.

Geometry→ Static / Transient Structural→ Mesh + Supports + Loads→ Solve→ Fatigue Tool

Stress-Life (High Cycle)

Used for >100,000 cycles where stress is the primary driver of fatigue.

Strain-Life (Low Cycle)

Used for <100,000 cycles where plastic deformation (strain) is significant.

Mean Stress Theories

Goodman
Brittle materials · Default
Conservative. Mean stress reduces fatigue strength linearly. Used for structural brackets, steel, cast iron.
Soderberg
High-yield materials · Most conservative
Replaces ultimate strength with yield strength. Suitable where safety is paramount — internal structures.
Gerber
Ductile materials · Rotating parts
Accurately represents curved mean stress–fatigue relationship for ductile materials (rocket shafts, engines).
ASME Elliptical
Industry standards · Tanks / Vessels
Combination of safety and reality. Often used for industry standard applications and pressure vessels.

Stress Component for Fatigue

  • Ductile Materials — Absolute Maximum Principal Stress
  • Brittle Materials — Signed Von-Mises Stress
Strength Factor (Kf): Typically 1, but varies based on surface finish, size, and problem statement.
